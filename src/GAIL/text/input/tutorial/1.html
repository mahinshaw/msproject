Tutorial Part I: Introduction to Screen

The top of the left-hand column is labeled Problem. This area contains one or more problems for you to work on. To solve a problem you must create arguments for and/or against the hypothesis given in the problems. The sections beneath Problem contain components of arguments: Hypotheses, Data from Medical Records, and Connections. You can drag these into the argument building workspace to build an argument. These are described in more detail in the section, How to build arguments.

The argument building workspace is the large white space in the center and right-hand side of the screen. You can drag argument components from the left-hand column into this area to build arguments. It has scroll bars on the side and bottom to allow you to see the entire workspace. In addition, you may need to use the toolbar in the upper right-hand side of the workspace. It has the following tools:

  • The eraser icon can be used to delete components of an argument from the workspace.
  • The & icon can be used to create a link between two or more components that means that the linked components are conjoined (jointly necessary).
  • The light bulb icon can be used to create a hypothesis in your own words instead of dragging one from the left-hand side of the screen.
  • The book icon allows you to view a tutorial.
  • The arrow icon can be used to submit your final answers.

If you forget what any of the icons is used for, you can move your mouse over it to see a tooltip.

Below the argument building workspace are two windows that provide different types of information:

  • Pedigree window: The pedigree window shows the patient's pedigree: a diagram used in genetics to show inheritance. In a pedigree, females are symbolized by circles and males by squares. A horizontal line connects the mother and father. A vertical line from a couple leads to each child of the couple.

    Shading is used to depict the number of copies of a gene mutation (0, 1, or 2) that a family member has in his or her cells. (Remember that everyone has two copies of each gene.) A question mark in a circle or rectangle means that the number is currently unknown. A circle or rectangle that is completely shaded represents having two mutated copies of a gene. Half shading represents having one mutated copy (and one normal copy) of a gene. No shading represents having no mutated copies (two normal copies) of a gene.

  • Workspace description: By clicking on the Refresh icon (circular arrow) in the upper right-hand corner of this window, you can see a description in English of the arguments that are currently in the workspace. If you change the workspace, you can click on Refresh again.

  • Chat: The chat window allows you to communicate with a teaching assistant who can answer your questions and give feedback if requested by you. Type your message in the area at the bottom of the chat window. Your messages and the assistant's replies will appear in the space above it.
